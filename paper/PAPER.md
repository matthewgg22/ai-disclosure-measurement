## Abstract

In U.S. 10-K filings, the phrase "artificial intelligence" spread from 0.8% of filers in
2001 to 50.7% in 2025. Over the same period, cheap marketing vocabulary ("AI-powered",
"AI-driven") reached 14.6% of filers while costly build vocabulary (large language model,
training compute, fine-tuning) stayed near 1.8%. Software (SIC-73) fell from a roughly 57%
(2018) peak share of AI mentions to 27% (2025) as the label diffused across sectors.
Measuring capability with audited R&D, the R&D-intensity premium of AI-labeled filers over
the market compressed toward zero across 2018–2024 (+0.036 to +0.009) even as the
AI-labeled pool grew from 37 firms (2015) to about 2,400 (2024); the 2015 benchmark is a
small sample (n = 37) and is flagged as such. Together these describe a label whose
information content about real capability decayed as it proliferated — a lemons
precondition (Akerlof, 1970) — measured entirely at the market level from public data, with
no claim about any individual issuer.

**Keywords:** securities disclosure, AI washing, textual analysis, EDGAR, information content.

## 1. Introduction

A word in a securities filing is supposed to carry information. When a company writes that
it uses "artificial intelligence," a reader — an investor, an analyst, a regulator —
reasonably infers something about what the company does. This paper asks a narrow empirical
question: over the last quarter century, has the "AI" label in U.S. 10-K filings continued
to carry information about a firm's actual capability, or has it become cheap talk?

The question matters for disclosure policy. Securities disclosure works only if the
vocabulary of disclosure stays tethered to substance. When a term becomes free to use — when
saying "AI" costs nothing and signals nothing — it stops helping investors discriminate and
starts helping issuers pool with more capable firms. Regulators have recently named this
pattern "AI washing" and brought enforcement actions against advisers who overstated their
use of the technology (SEC, 2024). The claims in those cases are issuer-specific and are not
the subject of this paper. What this paper provides is the market-level backdrop against
which such claims sit: how far, and in what direction, the language of AI in filings has
moved relative to its substance.

The contribution is deliberately modest and deliberately clean. It is a set of four
aggregate measurements over the full 10-K corpus, each reproducible from public data with a
single command, and each reported with its own disciplining caveat. The unit of analysis is
the filing corpus and named sectors — never an individual company.

## 2. Data

All measurements draw on public sources. Filing-level text counts come from the SEC's EDGAR
full-text search (FTS), which indexes filings from 2001 onward. Because FTS caps its reported
total at 10,000 results, it cannot serve as a denominator once a term becomes common; for the
count of distinct 10-K filers per year we instead use the EDGAR full-index master file, which
is the complete filing-level population. Audited financials — research and development
expense and revenue — come from the SEC's XBRL `frames` API, which returns a whole-market
cross-section for a given concept and year. Factor and market data used elsewhere in the
project are not used here; this paper is text and audited fundamentals only.

Phrases are matched as quoted strings so that phrase search does not pick up token false
positives (electrical "transformers", sales "agents"). All counts are cached locally and are
regenerated by the accompanying pipeline. Because EDGAR accrues new filings over time,
absolute counts drift; the stable objects are the shares and ratios reported below.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 The label went everywhere (Figure 1)

The share of 10-K filers using the phrase "artificial intelligence" rose from 0.79% in 2001
to 50.69% in 2025. The rise is not linear: it is flat through roughly 2016, then steepens
sharply after 2017 and again after 2022. A second phrase, "generative AI," is essentially
absent through 2022 and then appears in 13.37% of filers by 2025 — evidence that the
vocabulary itself churns, with new terms entering as older ones saturate. By 2025 a majority
of public-company annual reports invoke the label.

### 3.2 The substance did not follow (Figure 2)

Adoption of the word is not adoption of the thing. Splitting AI vocabulary into a *marketing*
register ("AI-powered", "AI-driven", "AI-native") and a *build* register (large language
model, foundation model, training compute, fine-tuning, retrieval-augmented generation), the
two diverge. By 2025 marketing vocabulary reaches 14.55% of filers while build vocabulary —
the language a firm uses when it is actually training or deploying models — stays at 1.77%.
In 2001 both were near zero (0.05% and 0.32%). The widening gap between the cheap register
and the costly one is the population-level fingerprint of a label outrunning its substance.

Two cautions. First, the bucket values are summed shares of their member phrases and are
therefore upper bounds, since a single filing can use several phrases; the robust object is
the *divergence* between the two registers, not the level of either. Second, and more
fundamental, a population ratio cannot classify any individual filing: that a marketing
phrase is common and a build phrase is rare says nothing about whether any particular company
is or is not doing real work. This is an ecological-inference boundary, and it is a boundary
this paper does not cross.

### 3.3 The label spread beyond software (Figure 3)

If the label tracked capability, its sectoral home would be stable. It is not. Software
(2-digit SIC 73) accounted for a peak of roughly 57% of all AI mentions in 2018; by 2025 that
share had fallen to 27% as the label diffused into every sector of the economy. The early
2000s values are noisy — there were few AI-mentioning filers then, so the denominators are
small — and the honest reading is therefore the post-2018 decline from the peak, not a smooth
twenty-five-year slide. The direction is nonetheless clear: the label has migrated out of the
industry that produces the technology and into industries that consume, or merely invoke, it.

### 3.4 The label's link to real capability faded (Figure 4)

The sharpest test asks whether AI-labeled firms still look like firms that do research. Using
audited R&D — hard to fabricate, and not reported at all by the financial holding companies
that dilute the pool — two things fall as the label proliferates. First, the share of
AI-labeled filers reporting *any* R&D declines from 51.4% (2015) to 42.4% (2024). Second, the
median R&D-intensity (R&D over revenue) *premium* of AI-labeled firms over the all-filer
baseline compresses toward zero: +0.036 in 2018, +0.022 in 2021, +0.009 in 2024. Over the
same window the AI-labeled pool grows from 37 firms (2015) to 278 (2018), 744 (2021), and
2,396 (2024).

The 2015 benchmark rests on only 37 firms and its negative point estimate (−0.059) is not
reliable; it is flagged as a small sample and the defensible reading is the monotone
compression across 2018–2024, when the pool is large. That compression is a lemons
precondition in the sense of Akerlof (1970): as a costless label spreads, the average quality
of the firms carrying it converges toward the market, and the label's power to separate
capable firms from the rest erodes.

## 4. Interpretation

Read together, the four measurements describe a single arc. A label that once appeared almost
exclusively in software firms, in a handful of filings, has become a majority phenomenon that
appears everywhere; its cheap register has raced ahead of its costly one; and its correlation
with audited research capability has compressed toward zero. The information content of the
"AI" label — its ability, on average, to tell a reader something about what a firm can do —
has fallen.

It is important to be precise about what this does and does not establish. It is a statement
about the *average* informativeness of a word across a large corpus. It does **not** identify
which firms are overstating, it does **not** quantify investor harm, and it does **not**
establish causation. Claims of those kinds require issuer-level evidence and belong to a
different body of work; they are outside the scope of, and the data used in, this paper. The
value of a market-level result is precisely that it is general: it establishes the condition
under which issuer-level "AI washing" becomes both easy and hard to detect — easy because the
label is free, hard because the label no longer separates.

The finding also sits alongside a prior literature on the market's reaction to fashionable
corporate language. Cooper, Dimitrov, and Rau (2001) documented that dot-com era name changes
earned abnormal returns unrelated to any change in business. The pattern here is the textual,
filing-level analogue a generation later: the adoption of a hot label at a pace that
outstrips the adoption of the underlying capability.

## 5. Limitations

The measurement is phrase-based and therefore inherits the limits of dictionary methods in
textual analysis (Loughran and McDonald, 2011): phrase lists are not exhaustive, and language
evolves. EDGAR full-text search caps its totals, which is why the denominator comes from the
master index rather than from FTS. The marketing and build buckets are summed upper bounds,
so their levels overstate; only the divergence is interpreted. Sector shares in the early
2000s rest on small samples. The informativeness test uses four benchmark years and audited
R&D, which not all firms report; the 2015 point in particular is a small sample. And because
EDGAR accrues filings continuously, absolute counts drift over time even as the reported
shapes remain stable.

## 6. Conclusion

Between 2001 and 2025 the "AI" label in U.S. securities filings went from a rare, software-
specific term to a majority phenomenon spanning every sector, while the costly vocabulary of
actually building AI stayed flat and the label's correlation with audited research capability
compressed toward zero. The label's information content decayed as it proliferated. For
disclosure policy the implication is not that any particular claim is false, but that the
word itself has stopped doing the work disclosure relies on it to do. Restoring that work — so
that saying "AI" once again means something — is a measurement problem before it is an
enforcement one, and the measurements here are offered as a public, reproducible baseline.
